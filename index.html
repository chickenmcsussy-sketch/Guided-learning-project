Interview: Case File

Four questions from my interview with Mr Kannan, treated as evidence to investigate rather than quotes to memorise.

Question

How do the CPU, RAM, storage and motherboard work together when a program runs?

Evidence

He mapped it onto the human body: the CPU is the brain, the motherboard is the nervous system, and RAM is memory — better RAM means more can be held and recalled.

Industry Insight

A biological analogy isn't decoration — it's how an experienced engineer keeps a complex system reasoned about correctly under pressure.

Reflection

The analogy made an abstract block diagram feel physical for the first time.

Application

I used the same brain / nervous system / memory model to structure the Hardware Explorer above.

Question

What's the most important thing students should understand to go deeper?

Evidence

He compared it to cooking: know the basic recipe well, and you can adapt it to any taste. Strong fundamentals let you branch into any specialisation later.

Industry Insight

Depth in fundamentals, not breadth of tools, is what he values most in people entering the field.

Reflection

I'd been trying to learn many tools shallowly instead of building one solid base.

Application

I reordered my own study plan to prioritise core architecture before networking or PCB design.

Question

How do you troubleshoot a computer that fails to boot?

Evidence

A fixed order: power supply first, then motherboard indicators, then beep codes, then reseat the RAM, then disconnect external devices, then BIOS, then storage.

Industry Insight

Systematic elimination, not guessing, is what separates a professional diagnosis from trial and error.

Reflection

I assumed troubleshooting starts wherever seems obvious. There's actually a defined sequence.

Application

That exact sequence became one of my four Engineering Principles below.

Question

Which emerging hardware technologies will matter most?

Evidence

He expects AI to stay at its peak for the next 3-5 years, with GPU and RAM demand (companies like Nvidia) leading it, before the curve shifts to quantum and later photonic computing.

Industry Insight

Today's RAM and GPU demand is driven by AI workloads, not just gaming — a different explanation than I expected.

Reflection

This gave me an actual timeframe to think in, instead of just a list of buzzwords.

Application

I referenced this trend directly in the RAM and GPU popups in the Hardware Explorer.

08

Engineering Principles

Four principles I pulled directly out of Mr Kannan's advice, not generic ones.

"If your fundamentals are strong, you can branch into anything" — like knowing a base recipe well enough to adapt it to any taste. He builds every recommendation on this first.

He pointed out that most people default to the software side and overlook hardware, even though real-world problem solving is often hardware too — and was glad to see someone focus on architecture.

His boot-failure troubleshooting followed a fixed sequence: power, motherboard indicators, beep codes, RAM, external devices, BIOS, storage — elimination in order, not guessing.

His advice: build curiosity first — try a Raspberry Pi project, connect and control something physical. He also named cloud computing as the one area he wishes he'd learned earlier.
